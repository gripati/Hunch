# UI/UX & Motion Playbook — AURORA

How to design a site that expresses the **spirit** of the specific product, looks like the work of a **discerning human designer** (not an AI template), and feels **full-juice, full-polish** — without sacrificing usability, performance, or the conversion path. Read alongside `frontend-design/SKILL.md`. Snippets are starting points; tune everything to the brief.

This file is the **philosophy and the budget**; the ready-to-paste code lives in **`references/motion-toolkit.md`** (the Juice Toolkit) — ~30 vanilla CSS/JS recipes (magnetic buttons, spotlight/3D-tilt cards, border-beam, count-up tickers, text reveal/scramble/typewriter, aurora/mesh backgrounds, dot-grid cursor spotlight, scroll-stacking cards, clip-path reveals, marquees, cursor trails, dynamic-island morphs). §5 below tells you *what* motion the page needs and *how much*; the toolkit gives you the *exact* implementation.

The single most important rule: **a visitor should not be able to tell this was generated by an AI** — and the two ways to fail that are being **too static** *and* being **over-juiced**. The sections that protect against the first failure are [Reject the default direction](#2-reject-the-default-direction), [Type](#type-no-novelty-fonts-clean-modern-grotesques-used-with-craft), [Original components](#4-original-components-not-generic-widgets), and [The motion system](#5-the-motion-system--juice--polish). Spend real effort there.

## 1. Find the spirit

Before a single color, name the **one feeling** the design should give and the **world** it comes from. Derive it from the Brief:

- **METAMORPH's transformation** — the After identity → the emotional destination the design should embody.
- **EMBER's dominant emotion** — relief / pride / security / ambition / play → the mood.
- **EDGE's tone tokens** — the voice → the visual register (technical, warm, premium, raw, playful).
- **The product's world** — its materials, instruments, artifacts, interfaces, vernacular → where distinctive, non-generic choices come from.

Write one line: *"This design should make a [ICP] feel [emotion], like [world metaphor]."* The design serves *that* line — never a generic template.

## 2. Reject the default direction

An AI asked to "design a landing page" gravitates to a tiny set of **defaults**. They are competent, and they are instantly recognizable as machine-made. Your job is to **not ship the default**.

### The default tells (recognize them so you can avoid them)

**Default layout** — the universal AI template: a centered hero (eyebrow + big headline + one-line subhead + two buttons) → a row of exactly three identical feature cards → a "how it works" 3-step row → a pricing table → an FAQ → a centered final CTA. If your wireframe is *that*, you've defaulted.

**Default aesthetics** — the three or four looks AI reaches for first:
- *Dark SaaS:* near-black background, one neon/violet gradient accent, glassmorphism cards, a glow behind the hero.
- *Soft startup:* white/very-light background, everything rounded, pastel gradient blobs, one bright accent.
- *Editorial serif:* cream background, a big serif display, terracotta/sage accents, thin hairlines.
- *Gradient-everything:* purple→blue gradients on buttons, text, and blobs.

**Default components** — gradient-border cards ×3, pill badges, check-mark-in-a-circle bullet lists, a generic line-icon set, the same "stat trio."

### The forcing function

1. **Brainstorm at least three distinct directions** for the product's spirit. The *first* idea you have is almost always the default — **set it aside on purpose** and develop a less-obvious one.
2. **Borrow the organizing idea from the product's real world** — its instruments, materials, interfaces, documents, vernacular — not from "what a landing page looks like." (A dev tool → a control panel / terminal. A finance tool → a ledger / statement. A kids' product → building blocks. A writing tool → a manuscript / margin notes.)
3. **Commit to one unconventional structural decision** so the page has a point of view: an asymmetric or editorial layout, an unexpected grid (bento, broken-grid), a diagrammatic or interface-as-hero instead of a stock headline-and-buttons hero, a strong typographic moment, a sticky side-rail, numbered sections. One bold, justified move beats ten safe ones.
4. **Take exactly one real aesthetic risk** and execute it cleanly. Risk-free design reads as templated.

The goal is not "weird for its own sake" — it's a *considered* direction a thoughtful human designer would choose for *this* product, that an AI wouldn't reach for first.

## 3. The design language (tokens)

**Pick the design language first — from the catalog of 50 in `design-languages.md`.** Choose the one (or a tasteful two-way mix) whose *spirit* fits this product, rejecting the obvious category default. The chosen language is a **style card** that sets not just color but the **whole material system and the page's anatomy — the hero concept, button shape, panel/card design, title/type idiom, motion personality, and even the ad-creative video idiom** — not only the palette. **These must visibly vary from project to project** — a neo-brutalist product gets 0px corners, thick borders, and hard offset shadows; a soft consumer app gets 24px corners and pillowy shadows; a glassmorphism product gets frosted translucent panels. Sameness of radius/shadow across projects is itself an AI tell. And anywhere a real platform or company appears, use its **real logo** (see `design-languages.md` and `brand-assets.md`).

Produce a compact token system from the chosen language. Define everything as CSS custom properties so the whole site re-themes from one place.

### Color
Derive from the spirit (section 1), not from the default palettes (section 2). Cover: a base/background, 1–2 surface levels, primary text + a muted text, a **primary accent** (CTAs/links), an optional **secondary accent** (highlights), and semantic colors (success/danger) used sparingly. Pick a palette with a point of view (an unexpected but coherent accent, a considered neutral that isn't pure-gray, a duotone drawn from the product's world). Check contrast (body text ≥ 4.5:1).

```css
:root{
  --bg:#__; --surface:#__; --surface-2:#__;
  --text:#__; --muted:#__;
  --accent:#__; --accent-2:#__;
  --ok:#__; --danger:#__;
}
```

### Type — no novelty fonts; clean modern grotesques used with craft

**Avoid the AI font tells.** Do **not** reach, by default, for a decorative serif on the hero (Playfair Display, Fraunces, Cormorant, DM Serif) or a trendy "designer" display face that has become AI shorthand (Space Grotesk, Clash Display, Cabinet Grotesk, Syne). A novelty typeface is the most common AI tell.

**Use a clean, modern, highly legible grotesque sans as the workhorse.** **Inter** is the reference standard; equally professional Inter-family-style peers: **Geist, Hanken Grotesk, Schibsted Grotesk, Plus Jakarta Sans, Albert Sans, IBM Plex Sans**. Use one of these for the bulk of the UI — body *and* headings.

**Get distinctiveness from *usage*, not from the font's name.** A clean grotesque becomes characterful through craft:
- a real **type scale** (a deliberate modular ratio, confident jumps between sizes),
- strong **weight contrast** (e.g. 400 body against 700–800 display),
- tight **negative letter-spacing** on large display sizes (e.g. `-0.02em` to `-0.03em`),
- **optical sizing** where available, generous body line-height (~1.6), and intentional measure (~60–70ch).

```css
:root{
  --font-sans:"Inter","Geist",system-ui,-apple-system,"Segoe UI",sans-serif; /* workhorse */
  --font-mono:"JetBrains Mono","IBM Plex Mono",ui-monospace,monospace;       /* only if justified */
}
h1{font-family:var(--font-sans);font-weight:800;letter-spacing:-.025em;font-size:clamp(2.4rem,6vw,4.2rem);line-height:1.04}
body{font-family:var(--font-sans);font-weight:400;line-height:1.62}
```

**A second face only with a real reason** — a **mono** for code/data/technical products (justified by the product's world, e.g. a terminal), or one characterful accent face used sparingly for a single role. Never a decorative serif dropped on the hero by default.

**The point:** a neutral, modern font + an *original layout and components* beats a trendy font + a generic template. Spend your novelty budget on composition and components (sections 2 and 4), not on the typeface.

### Spacing & rhythm
A consistent spacing scale and generous, intentional section padding (`clamp()` for fluid spacing). Whitespace and rhythm carry as much personality as color.

### The signature element
Choose **one** memorable element drawn from the product's world that the page is remembered by (a live demo, an animated diagram of the core mechanic, a real terminal/command, an interactive toy). Spend your boldness here; keep everything around it quiet.

## 4. Original components, not generic widgets

Every repeated element should look **chosen and made for this product**, not pulled from the component library every AI uses. Build a small set of distinctive, reusable elements and apply them consistently. For each common element: the generic default → a more original approach.

- **Lists / bullets** — *not* the check-mark-in-a-circle list. Use a custom marker that *means something* for the brand: a terminal `›`/`$`, a numbered tick, a small diagram glyph, or a left **rule with a colored cap**. Or restructure entirely as a definition list, a two-column `label → value` grid, or an annotated list with side notes.
- **Cards** — *not* three identical gradient-border rounded boxes. Vary size and weight (a **bento / asymmetric grid**), give a **signature detail** (a corner index number `01`, a hairline ruled header, a monospaced label, a hover that reveals), or make cards resemble the product's real **artifacts** (a file, a ticket, a terminal pane, a receipt, an index card).
- **Steps / progress** — *not* a plain horizontal bar or a flat 3-across row. Make it expressive: a **numbered vertical timeline** with connecting rules and state, a segmented **stepper**, a progress UI that matches the product, or a **count-up** tied to a meaningful number.
- **Stats** — *not* three centered big-number + label columns. Try an **inline annotated figure**, a **comparison bar**, a before/after element, or a labeled callout with a real source.
- **Icons** — *not* the default generic line-icon set used identically everywhere. Pick one intentional, consistent icon style (or small custom glyphs), and make them feel like one family. If using emoji as markers, only when genuinely on-brand.
- **Section markers / eyebrows** — *not* the same centered eyebrow + heading every time. Use a system: **numbered sections** (`01 / 02 / 03`), a ruled label, an asymmetric heading block, a running side-label.
- **Dividers / texture** — replace flat gaps with intentional structure: a hairline grid, a baseline rule, a subtle dotted/graph-paper texture drawn from the product's world.

Aim for **2–3 genuinely custom, branded components** the visitor will remember — not a page of defaults with one accent color swapped.

```css
/* example: a branded marker instead of a check-circle bullet */
.list{list-style:none;padding:0;display:grid;gap:.7rem}
.list li{position:relative;padding-left:1.6rem}
.list li::before{content:"›";position:absolute;left:0;color:var(--accent);font-family:var(--font-mono);font-weight:600}

/* example: a card with a signature corner index, not a gradient border */
.card{position:relative;background:var(--surface);border:1px solid var(--line);border-radius:14px;padding:28px}
.card[data-idx]::after{content:attr(data-idx);position:absolute;top:16px;right:18px;font-family:var(--font-mono);font-size:.8rem;color:var(--muted)}
```

## 5. The motion system — juice + polish

Motion is designed, not sprinkled. **Juice** = satisfying feedback and life (things respond, settle, reward interaction). **Polish** = consistent timing, natural easing, restraint so it reads as crafted, not busy. An orchestrated moment lands harder than scattered effects — and over-animation is itself an AI tell.

**The old default was too thin.** A page that only fades sections up and lifts buttons on hover is the *static* tell the visitor feels even if they can't name it: dead hovers, a headline that just appears, printed-looking numbers, a flat single-color void behind the hero. The bar now is the level of the best motion libraries — [Skiper UI](https://skiper-ui.com), [Cult UI](https://www.cult-ui.com), [Style UI](https://www.styleui.dev), [Watermelon UI](https://ui.watermelon.sh) — re-expressed in vanilla code. **All the implementations are in `references/motion-toolkit.md`.** This section is the *spec*: what to ship, and how much.

### The juice contract — what a finished Hunch page must do

Not a menu; a **contract**. Every site ships all five:

1. **Baseline, always** (`motion-toolkit.md` §0) — the reveal harness that drives *both* the hero load sequence (headline → sub → CTA → signature, staggered ~90ms) *and* scroll reveals, plus the motion tokens and the reduced-motion switch. One observer, `data-reveal` variants (up / scale / blur / clip-wipe), staggered children. This replaces the old fade-up-only reveal.
2. **Every interactive element responds** (§2, §3) — buttons get press physics; the primary CTA gets one signature (magnetic, shimmer, or border-beam); cards get at least a hover lift *or* a cursor-tracked spotlight. **No dead hovers** anywhere.
3. **One ambient background** (§4) — behind the hero or a key section: an animated aurora/mesh, a dot-grid cursor-spotlight, a conic beam, or a cursor-reactive tint. Exactly one — never two competing for the eye.
4. **One scroll-choreography move** (§5) — the page performs as you read: a sticky nav that condenses + a scroll-progress bar at minimum; a stacking-card section, a clip-path image reveal, or scroll-linked text as a bonus.
5. **Exactly one signature moment** (§1, §6, or the language's assigned recipe) — the animation the visitor *remembers*, and which *demonstrates the product*: a typing terminal, a count-up dashboard, a before→after drag slider, an image cursor trail, a dynamic-island morph. Spend the boldness here; keep everything around it quiet.

### Choosing the recipes — by design language

Don't pick motion at random. The chosen design language (from `design-languages.md`) determines the *personality* of the motion. Use the **[Signature juice by family](motion-toolkit.md#signature-juice-by-family)** map in the toolkit — it routes each of the 50 languages to its family's recipes so the motion matches the material:

- **Hard / bold** (neo-brutalism, gig-poster, pop-art) → slam-in words (no soft easing), stacking cards, hard press. Snappy, abrupt.
- **Tech / terminal** (dark techno, terminal, cyberpunk, blueprint, data) → typewriter, scramble/decode, count-ups, dot-grid cursor spotlight, border-beam buttons.
- **Soft / premium** (luxury, art deco, fashion-photo, Scandinavian) → shimmer text, clip-path image reveals, shine sweeps, subtle aurora + grain.
- **Playful / organic** (claymorphism, liquid, casual-game, Y2K, soft) → magnetic CTA, cursor trail, squash press, 3D tilt, floating orbs.
- **3D / depth / holo** (3D-render, spatial, holographic, glassmorphism, aurora-mesh) → 3D tilt cards, parallax layers, spotlight cards, cursor-reactive tint.
- **Editorial / type** (kinetic type, editorial, Swiss, newspaper) → word/char reveal, scroll-linked text highlight, masked line reveals.
- **Refined SaaS / minimal / bento** (the tasteful default, done *well*) → count-up stats, spotlight cards, stacking sections, shimmer button.

### The signature moment demonstrates the product

The one bold animation should *show what the thing does*, not decorate: a dev tool → a terminal that types a real command; an analytics tool → numbers that count up into a live dashboard; a transformation story → a before→after drag slider (METAMORPH's After made literal); a creative tool → an image cursor trail. One memorable moment, executed cleanly (toolkit §1, §6).

### Restraint is the other half

Add the baseline + one ambient + one scroll move + one signature — then **stop**. Two backgrounds fighting, every word animating, a bounce on everything: that's the *over-juiced* tell, as machine-made as the static one. The budget and the full guardrails (GPU-only, no CLS, `will-change` discipline, throttled scroll/pointer, lazy off-screen init, reduced-motion, ≥44px hit areas) are in `motion-toolkit.md` §7 — treat them as pass/fail.

## 6. The quality floor

Polish *includes* the floor. Every motion and layout must hold up:

- **Reduced motion** — disable non-essential animation under the user's preference:
  ```css
  @media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce){
    *{animation:none!important}
    .reveal{opacity:1!important;transform:none!important;transition:none}
    html{scroll-behavior:auto}
  }
  ```
- **No layout shift** — animate only `transform`/`opacity`; reserve space for images/embeds (set width/height); never animate `width`/`height`/`top`/`left` of in-flow content.
- **Performance (coordinate with SUMMIT)** — GPU-cheap animations, deferred non-critical JS, compressed images, lean hero for fast LCP. Beauty keeps Core Web Vitals green.
- **Accessibility** — visible keyboard focus (`:focus-visible`), semantic HTML, contrast, hit areas ≥ ~44px, motion never the only way to convey meaning. Distinctive components must still be operable and legible.
- **Responsive** — designed down to mobile; test the hero, the signature, the custom components, and the CTAs at small widths.

## 7. Self-critique — the AI-tell audit

Before handing the design to ATLAS, look with fresh, critical eyes:

- **Default direction?** Is this the first/most obvious aesthetic for this product? If yes, redesign from a less-obvious one (section 2).
- **Template hero?** Does it match the universal AI layout (centered headline + subhead + two buttons → 3 cards → pricing)? Break at least one part of that mold.
- **Default components?** Are the bullets check-circles, the cards gradient-border trios, the steps a flat bar, the icons a generic set? Replace them with original, branded ones (section 4).
- **Novelty font doing the work?** Is character coming from a trendy typeface, or from a clean modern grotesque used with craft (scale, weight, tracking)? Prefer the latter (section 3).
- **Too static?** Load the page and scroll it. Does it *move* — a hero that assembles, live numbers, responsive hovers, an ambient background, something that performs as you read? Or is it flat sections, dead hovers, and printed-looking stats? If it doesn't feel alive, it fails the juice contract (section 5 + `motion-toolkit.md`).
- **Over-juiced?** The opposite failure: everything bounces, two backgrounds fight, every word animates, the eye never rests. Cut back to one ambient, one scroll move, one signature; quiet the rest.
- **One real point of view?** Is there a single bold, justified decision a human designer would be proud of — or is it uniformly safe?
- **The glance test:** would a discerning designer recognize this as AI-generated at a glance? If yes, it is not done.
- **Floor:** reduced-motion respected, focus visible, no layout shift, fast, responsive — and the design still serves the message, the proof, and the CTA, never the reverse.
