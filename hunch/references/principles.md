# The 10 Principles — Operational Playbook

Where `agents.md` defines *who* applies each principle, this file is *how* to apply it well, with do/don't examples. Read the entry for a principle when its agent is working. Examples use a running sample product — **a freelance-invoicing app** — purely to make the technique concrete; swap in the real product.

---

## 1. People buy with emotions and justify it with logic — *EMBER*

**The idea.** The decision to buy is made emotionally, then defended rationally. If you only argue with logic, you never trigger the decision. If you only stir emotion, the buyer can't justify the spend to themselves (or their boss/partner) and backs out.

**Why it works.** Emotion creates motion; logic creates permission. People feel first, then look for reasons.

**How to apply.** Open and recur on emotion (relief, pride, security). Cluster logic at the decision points — pricing, guarantee, the final CTA — where the buyer is rationalizing. Give them defensible ammunition: ROI, time saved, the guarantee, hard specifics.

- ✅ *Emotional:* "Stop dreading the end of the month." → *Logical (near price):* "Pays for itself the first time it recovers one late invoice — average recovered: $1,900/yr."
- ❌ "Our app uses double-entry ledgers and OAuth2." (All logic, no want.)

---

## 2. Sell the transformation, not the product — *METAMORPH*

**The idea.** Nobody wants the product. They want who they *become* once it has done its job. Sell the Before→After.

**Why it works.** The product is a cost and a hassle; the transformation is the prize. Buyers pay for outcomes and identities, not tools.

**How to apply.** Lead with the After identity, anchored against the Before pain. Make the offer the *bridge*, not the hero. Features appear only as evidence the transformation is real.

- ✅ "From the founder who sends invoices late and looks scattered — to the one who never misses a payment and reads as buttoned-up to every client."
- ❌ "A modern invoicing platform with templates and reminders." (Sells the tool, not the change.)

---

## 3. Easier to sell something that solves a pain than something that targets desire — *LANCE*

**The idea.** Pain is a stronger, more urgent buying trigger than desire. A bleeding problem gets a wallet open faster than a nice-to-have aspiration.

**Why it works.** Pain is present-tense and urgent; desire is future and deferrable. People pay now to stop hurting; they delay paying to maybe feel better someday.

**How to apply.** Lead with the sharpest pain and its cost of inaction. Where the product serves both a pain and a desire, put pain first in the page, desire second as upside.

- ✅ "You lost a client last quarter because your invoice got buried — and you didn't notice for six weeks." (Present pain + consequence.)
- ❌ "Imagine a beautifully organized finance dashboard." (Pleasant desire, no urgency.)

---

## 4. Solving an existing demand beats creating a new one — *COMPASS*

**The idea.** It's far cheaper to be the better answer to a question people already ask than to teach them a brand-new question. Ride demand; don't manufacture it.

**Why it works.** Educating a market into a new category is slow and expensive. Slotting into an existing search/intent is fast and cheap — the demand and vocabulary already exist.

**How to apply.** Find what the buyer already searches/buys/hacks to handle the pain. Use *their* words. Position as the better version of a thing they already seek, not a novel concept needing a 3-paragraph explanation.

- ✅ "The invoicing app that actually remembers to send and chase." (Better answer to an existing need.)
- ❌ "A revolutionary autonomous receivables-orchestration paradigm." (New category, high explanation cost.)

---

## 5. People care how it helps them, not the product — *BEACON*

**The idea.** The buyer's only question is "what's in it for me?" (WIIFM). Every feature must be translated into a benefit and then a felt outcome.

**Why it works.** Features describe the product; benefits describe the buyer's improved life. Buyers buy the latter.

**How to apply.** Run feature → benefit → outcome on everything. Always reach the outcome. Cut features that have no buyer benefit from the sales page.

- ✅ "Automatic reminders → you never chase a late payer again → you get paid ~9 days sooner and skip the awkward emails."
- ❌ "Includes automatic reminders." (Feature, no WIIFM.)

---

## 6. Use words that hit the ego and the fear at the same time — *EDGE*

**The idea.** The two strongest motivators are the desire for status (ego) and the fear of loss (fear). Hooks that hit *both at once* are the most powerful copy you can write.

**Why it works.** Ego pulls the buyer toward a better self-image; fear pushes them away from loss. One hook engaging both push and pull is hard to ignore.

**How to apply.** For the hero and key hooks, name the ego (what they want to be seen as) and the fear (what they dread losing), then fuse them in one line. Keep it honest — amplify real stakes — and specific (PRISM), never generic dread.

- ✅ "Stop looking unprofessional to the clients you fought to win." (Fear of losing status + ego of the hard-won client.)
- ❌ "Don't miss out — get organized today!" (Generic, hits neither axis sharply.)

---

## 7. Show social proof; the brain believes and creates the need — *CHORUS*

**The idea.** Seeing that others already got the result makes the brain believe the claim *and* manufacture the desire to have it too. Proof beside a claim converts the claim.

**Why it works.** Social proof is a mental shortcut for "this is safe and it works." Belief plus the sight of peers succeeding generates the need.

**How to apply.** Place relevant proof next to every major promise and CTA: numbers for scale, testimonials (from people like the ICP) for relatability, case studies for situational fit, logos/press for authority, guarantees as confidence. If you have no proof yet, use clearly-labeled placeholders and a collection plan — never fake it.

- ✅ Next to the headline claim: "1,240 freelancers got paid faster — average 9 days sooner." + a quote from a freelancer the ICP recognizes as a peer.
- ❌ A lonely testimonials carousel at the bottom, far from any ask.

---

## 8. Nothing is too expensive for the right customer — *VAULT*

**The idea.** Price is only "high" relative to the wrong buyer or an unclear value. Define the right customer precisely and price to the value they receive.

**Why it works.** For the right customer, the pain is expensive and the transformation is valuable — price becomes a rounding error against what they gain. For the wrong customer, any price feels like too much.

**How to apply.** Sharpen the ICP to a specific person for whom this is worth a lot. Quantify the value in their terms. Anchor the price (against the cost of the pain, the alternative, or the bundle value) so it reads as obviously worth it. Don't try to be cheap enough for everyone — filter to the right buyer.

- ✅ "Recovers ~$1,900/yr in late and missed invoices. It's $19/mo." (Value dwarfs price; the right buyer can't say no.)
- ❌ "Only $19! Cheapest invoicing app around!" (Competes on cheapness, attracts the wrong, price-sensitive buyer.)

---

## 9. Be specific with words and numbers; avoid generic — *PRISM*

**The idea.** Specifics are believable and memorable; generics are noise the buyer has heard a thousand times and tuned out.

**Why it works.** "Save time" could be 30 seconds or 30 hours — the brain can't picture it, so it discounts it. "Cut invoicing from 3 hours to 11 minutes" is vivid, credible, and sticks.

**How to apply.** Replace every vague claim with a number, a named thing, or a concrete before/after. Audit headlines, bullets, CTAs, and the offer hardest. When you don't know the exact figure, use an honest, clearly-estimated range — never invent precise-looking numbers.

- ✅ "Used by 1,240 freelancers in 38 countries; they get paid ~9 days sooner."
- ❌ "Trusted by businesses everywhere to save time and boost results."

---

## 10. The market isn't saturated; maybe the offer isn't good enough — *FORGE*

**The idea.** "The market is too crowded" is usually a misdiagnosis. If something isn't selling, the offer is more often the problem than the market. Fix the offer before blaming demand.

**Why it works.** Crowded markets still reward the strongest offer. A clearer outcome, a better stack, and real risk reversal beat "more of the same," even amid competitors.

**How to apply.** When tempted to turn up the copy volume, strengthen the offer instead: sharpen the core outcome, add stack elements that each kill an objection, add genuine risk reversal, name it. Make it so good the right customer feels slightly foolish refusing.

- ✅ "Get paid faster in 30 days or it's free — includes done-for-you reminder sequences and a late-payer recovery script. ($19/mo.)" (Outcome + stack + risk reversal.)
- ❌ "We're like the others, but we try harder." (Same offer, louder voice — won't move a crowded market.)

## Putting it together: the persuasion arc of a page

These principles aren't independent decorations; they form a sequence the reader moves through:

1. **Hit the pain** (P3) in the buyer's own words and existing language (P4) — earns attention.
2. **Promise the transformation** (P2) with an ego+fear hook (P6) — creates desire.
3. **Make them feel it** (P1 emotion), then **translate the how** into benefits/outcomes (P5) — builds the want and shows the path.
4. **Prove it** (P7) beside every claim — converts belief.
5. **Present an offer too good to refuse** (P10) to the **right customer at value-anchored pricing** (P8) — removes resistance.
6. **Justify it** (P1 logic) and keep **every word specific** (P9) throughout — lets them say yes and defend it.

That arc *is* the website blueprint. See `website-blueprint.md` for the section-by-section map.
